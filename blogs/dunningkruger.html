Everyone in the Comment Section Is an Expert Now

Mr Pipo Dunning kruger" by Nevit Dilmen (talk) is licensed under CC BY-SA 3.0.

Scroll through Instagram or TikTok and you'll notice something interesting — everyone's suddenly an expert. Nutritionists, psychologists and climate scientists all conveniently broadcasting their self-proclaimed expertise in front of their devices. The internet is, without a doubt, a place containing a wide range of sources that you can certainly learn from — I've learned plenty from it myself. However, it seems to me that the internet has also turned into a kind of classroom, where supposed facts are laid out for the viewers to consume — except nobody's quite sure who the teacher is.

We currently live in an age where confidence and authority implies competence. Notice the abundance of mildly (or even explicitly) click-baity titles: "The Morning Routine That Made Me Rich" when it's nothing more than an advertisement for their clothing product, "Fruits Are Bad For You" due to the sugar content of certain fruits, without addressing the nuance in the structure of sugars between fruits and processed sugars, and "Vaccines Are Bad For You", justified by a couple of anecdotal stories. The point is this: many claim such statements to be true without any real credibility behind them. This is where both psychology and logic start to matter.

The Dunning-Kruger Effect

In 1999, psychologists David Dunning and Justin Kruger discovered a cognitive bias now known as the Dunning-Kruger effect — that people who know very little about a topic tend to overestimate their understanding of it [1]. The irony is quite poetic: if you lack the knowledge on a particular topic, you also lack the ability to recognize how much knowledge you lack. Simply put, one requires expertise to realize the difficulty of expertise.

On platforms like YouTube, TikTok and Instagram, this very effect turns into a feature, not a bug. With just a sprinkle of confidence, short form structure and a trendy title, all of a sudden one's half-baked opinion on climate change not only outperforms peer-reviewed papers, but also turns into a profitable career. When that formula works, why would any stop?

Logical Fallacies

Another piece of this puzzle lies in how these ideas are put forth. The following highlights how shallow reasoning often sneaks in to seemingly factual statements. There are two kinds of fallacies that I find quite often: the appeal to authority and the slippery slope.

The appeal to authority fallacy occurs when something is claimed to be true because an "expert" said so. Social media thrives on this. For example, one might watch a fitness influencer telling them that a particular diet triumphs all other diets, and they might sit there and think to themselves: "This influencer has a six-pack, therefore his advice on diet must be true". Another example may be that a doctor says vaccines are bad, therefore it must be true — without acknowledging what kind of doctor they are. Even if the doctor were to be legitimate, unfortunately there are plenty of cases when credentials don't necessarily tell you what is actually true. The point is this, real expertise isn't about one's confidence or titles, it's about evidence. The fallacy here isn't in listening to experts; it's in failing to check which kind in particular.

Another all too common fallacy is the slippery slope. It argues that one small step will inevitably lead to a disastrous outcome. When explaining the slippery slope fallacy, I tend to point to the COVID19 pandemic, and how arguments were made against certain mandates. For example, "If they can make you take a shot of vaccine, they can make you take any drug they want". It's quite clear here that othe logic breaks down on inspection — one public health measure doesn't automatically lead to some form of dystopian bodily control. It's a leap built on fear rather than reason.

Does The Internet Reward Ignorance?

It's difficult to say for certain that the internet truly rewards ignorance, but it certainly rewards certainty. Algorirthms favor engagement, not nuance. Careful thinking, which often consists of gray areas and conditional statements, rarely goes viral. Complex questions, whether it be in politics or science, often don't have simple answers. However, if a confident influencer jumps in to tell the public that "They have been lying to us!", turns out the latter generates more views than the former. This illustrates the phenomenon where the Dunning-Kruger effect meets the social-media algorithms: confidence gets views, and views amplify confidence. The result is a worldwide ensemble of confident nonsense.

The Humility of Knowing

The more you learn about something, the more you become aware of what you don't know. From my perspective, humility may not be the current trend, but it's the foundation of genuine understanding. The solution to this isn't cynicism, but rather, curiosity. Next time you're met with a claim that bothers you, resist the urge to shout out counter arguments fueled by bias and emotion. Instead, ask questions, verify those claims and stop confusing someone speaking loudly for someone being right. Ultimately, the point of this blog isn't about "other" people — it's about all of us including myself. We've all had moments where we were overconfident in our understanding after learning very little. The challenge here is to remember that real understanding takes a lot of patience, humility and sometimes, the wisdom to just keep scrolling.
