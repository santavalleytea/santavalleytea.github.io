The Art of Useless Knowledge

"The Map of Mathematics" by Domain of Science

It's quite evident from the expositions on this website that I indulge in what is called pure mathematics or â€” as I like to describe it, math for the sake of math. To some, that may sound like a bunch of abstract nonsense: concepts with no clear use, and honestly, they're not entirely wrong.

I often find myself in conversations where someone asks me, "So what do you do with a math degree? or "What exactly do you learn?" — questions that make me pause and wonder how to answer without sounding like a man who's lost his mind.

Applied vs. Pure

Broadly speaking, mathematics can be split into two large areas: applied and pure. Applied mathematics is, as its name suggests, applicable — it finds use in various fields outside of mathematics such as physics, engineering, biology, computer science, finance and so on. In other words, it's the mathematics that helps model motion, optimize systems and make predictions.

On the other hand, pure mathematics exists largely for itself. It's the study of ideas independent from application such as algebraic structures in abstract algebra, or deformation of objects in topology, where the rather unusual statement that a coffee cup is "the same" as a donut somehow makes mathematical sense. At first glance, applied math appears far more useful... and it is. This is precisely what makes the question why study pure mathematics? both interesting and complex.

Why Study Something "Useless"?

It's honestly a fair question. We're taught from early on that mathematics finds its value due to its use — it helps engineers build bridges, computer scientists code algorithms and space engineers launch rockets. So why would anyone devote years of effort to understand something that doesn't seem to do any of those things?

For me, the answer is simple; curiosity. Pure mathematics is less about practicality and more about following a story — wanting to know where the idea goes from one point to another. Consider a formula that many have seen before: $$ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} $$ This expression, called the quadratic formula, lets us find solutions to polynomial equations. Now, think about something seemingly unrelated: a square.
The red dotted lines show what is called the lines of symmetry. In other words, you can rotate or reflect the square along these lines and still end up with the same shape — its structure is preserved.

Now what if I told you that this simple symmetry is connected to that formula above? This is where Galois theory comes into play: the study of how the symmetries of a structure (also known as groups) correspond to the solvability of polynomial equations. It's through this theory that we learn why equations of degree five and higher — such as \(x^5 - x + 1 = 0\) — do not have a universal formula like the quadratic does.

This is the kind of set up that draws people into pure mathematics. It begins with something familiar and ends up somewhere unexpected. Every proof feels to me like a narrative: a mystery set up, a journey through the reasoning via logic, and an ending that feels inevitable once you've seen it. It is worth noting that the example I gave above involves studying deeply abstract and complex mathematics, which takes quite a lot of time and effort to understand. However, it is extremely rewarding and one that I certainly enjoyed studying, looking back on it.

Beyond Usefulness

Despite what I said earlier, pure mathematics turns out to be a lot more useful than it presents itself to be. Abstract algebra is used throughout cryptography and computer graphics, topology finds its way into computer networks, data science and even image processing. But to me, these are not reasons to study pure mathematics — rather a side effect.

Most people who find interest in studying this field don't do so because it might one day help out with their day to day life. They do it because of the beauty of the ideas themselves — because these structures, proofs and patterns form a kind of art.

In The End

Perhaps the value of pure mathematics isn't in what it provides, but in what it represents. I like to see it as a way of celebrating curiosity — of asking questions simply because they exist and are worth asking. It's an act of imagination, of seeing a story in an abstract setting.

In a world where people are obsessed with productivity and tangible outcomes, pure mathematics offer something I consider to be rare: a reminder that some of the most meaningful pursuits have little to no purpose beyond itself — and that's precisely what makes them worthwhile.
